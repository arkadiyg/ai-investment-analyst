# PPM Review Rubric

A repeatable checklist for evaluating a private-fund offering document at family-office quality. Walk every section even if a finding is "not applicable" — explicitly noting absence is itself useful.

## 1. Documentation deficiencies (often the most material findings)

- **Fee table.** Look for a clear table with: management fee (rate + basis), acquisition fee, disposition fee, financing/refinancing fee, carried interest %, hurdle/catch-up structure, and the cap on operating-expense reimbursements. **A PPM that mentions "Carried Interest" / "Asset Management Fee" / "Sharing Ratio" without specifying the rate is a major red flag** — the LP cannot model net returns.
- **Exhibits and supporting documents.** Verify the Business Overview / Investment Strategy / Sources-and-Uses exhibit is in the document, not deferred to an external portal. A PPM that punts the business plan to "see investor portal" is treating the plan as mutable and outside the offering.
- **GP commitment.** Look for a dollar amount and percentage of total commitments. Most legitimate funds disclose this. Absence is a flag.
- **Audited financials and track record.** Note whether prior-fund returns are audited or "internally prepared by affiliates." Note whether *any* third-party verification was performed.
- **Sources and Uses.** Reconcile the math. Be alert to unusually large marketing-and-advertising lines, unspecified "operating costs" exceeding 10% of the raise, or footnoted "redeployed capital" figures that do not appear in Sources.
- **Counsel.** Almost always represents the Manager and Company; investors typically waive conflicts. Note this as standard but not benign.

## 2. Governance and LP protections

- **Voting.** Class A / LP voting rights — usually none. Check whether LPs can remove the Manager (and on what trigger), amend the LPA, or approve affiliate transactions.
- **Removal for cause.** "Cause" is often defined extremely narrowly (criminal conviction, Bad Actor disqualification only). Performance, fiduciary breach, gross negligence, and key-person events should ideally trigger removal too.
- **Drag-along / tag-along.** Drag-along held by the Manager with no LP consent is normal in private funds but worth flagging. Tag-along should be unconditional, not contingent on the Manager's drag.
- **Indemnification and exculpation.** Look for the carve-outs (knowing violation of law, willful misconduct, improper personal benefit). Check if indemnification can be funded by clawing back distributions to LPs — that is investor-unfriendly.
- **Side letters / MFN.** Note whether the Manager reserves the right to grant superior terms to selected investors and whether an MFN is offered.
- **Preferred-equity subordination.** Whether later capital can subordinate Class A in the waterfall.
- **Affiliate transactions.** Whether they are pre-approved, disclosed, subject to LPAC review, or unilaterally permitted.

## 3. Liquidity and capital mechanics

- **Open-ended / evergreen vs. closed-end.** Open-ended funds shift control over the timing of return of capital to the Manager.
- **Lock-up.** Note duration.
- **Redemption gates.** Note the percentage cap and whether it is fund-wide or per-investor; whether it resets and how often; whether the Manager has discretion to override.
- **Distribution discretion.** Note whether the preferred return is paid mandatorily or "at the Manager's discretion." Discretion turns the pref into an aspiration.
- **NAV and valuation mechanics.** Is there a third-party valuation agent? Or does the Manager itself compute AUM (often the redemption-gate denominator) "in good faith"? The latter is a structural conflict.
- **Redemption price formula.** If redemption price = invested capital + accrued unpaid pref, the LP gets no upside participation. The pref is the **maximum** outcome. This is often the most overlooked structural issue.
- **Transfer rights.** Almost always restricted; note any unusual conditions (Manager consent + counsel opinion at the LP's expense is common but burdensome).

## 4. Strategy and asset-class risk

- **Cash-flow profile.** Does the strategy generate operating income (rents, royalties) or is it pre-cash-flow (raw land, development, build-to-sell)? A high pref on pre-cash-flow assets is structurally demanding.
- **Leverage.** Note maximum LTV, recourse vs. non-recourse, cross-default and cross-collateralization.
- **Guaranties.** Whether the Manager or its affiliates provide bad-boy, completion, or repayment guaranties — and whether the Fund indemnifies them out of fund assets in distress.
- **Macro fit.** Is the strategy currently in or out of favor for the cycle? Compare the implied required gross return (pref + fees + carry + overhead) to current market comparables.
- **Concentration.** Single-asset, single-sponsor, single-geography exposure.

## 5. Sponsor and track record

- **Registration status.** Whether the Manager is a registered investment adviser (state or SEC) or an exempt reporting adviser. Investors lose Advisers Act / Investment Company Act protections if neither.
- **Public profile.** Look up the Sponsor's other businesses. A sponsor whose primary public business is an education / coaching / "guru" platform raises a coach-promoter concern: the investor pool may overlap with the customer pool, which the SEC has flagged as a pattern.
- **Prior funds.** Number of prior vehicles, vintages, sizes, status, and whether returns are audited.
- **Parallel and successor funds.** A sponsor with Fund I, Fund II, Fund III simultaneously raising creates real (not hypothetical) deal-allocation conflicts. Note whether the LPA addresses this.
- **Form D reconciliation.** Pull the most recent Form D / D-A from SEC EDGAR for the Fund's CIK. Compare actual capital raised and number of investors against the marketed offering size. A target raise that is 50× the actual is either failing or aspirational; flag and ask.
- **Litigation, regulatory action, "Bad Actor" history.** BBB, FINRA BrokerCheck, IAPD, and a basic web search.

## 6. Tax exposure

- **Phantom income.** Real-estate partnerships often allocate taxable income before cash distributions match it. Note explicit phantom-income disclosures.
- **K-1 timing.** The Manager's commitment (or lack thereof) to deliver K-1s before April 15.
- **UBTI / ECI.** Material for tax-exempt and non-U.S. investors. Note whether the Manager commits to minimize UBTI or qualify for the Fractions Rule, VCOC, or REOC exceptions.
- **REIT election.** Whether the Manager can convert the structure to a REIT mid-fund without LP consent.
- **State filings.** Multiple-state filings can be a real cost for the LP; note whether composite returns are filed.

## Findings that should always go on page one of the CIO memo

1. The fee structure is undisclosed (or fully disclosed and quantified, with a worked example).
2. The business plan is delivered as a formal exhibit to the PPM (or it is not).
3. The pref is the LP's maximum outcome, not a floor (or the LP shares in upside via carry).
4. The marketed raise reconciles to SEC Form D actuals (or it does not).
5. The sponsor's primary business is investment management (or something else).

If any of those five flips negative, the deal is unlikely to clear a family-office IC without remediation.

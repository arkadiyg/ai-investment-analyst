## Buffett Analyst Signal Report — FTLS

**Signal:** neutral (leaning bearish)
**Confidence:** 60

### Security Identification
- **Name:** First Trust Long/Short Equity ETF
- **Ticker:** FTLS (NYSE Arca)
- **Type:** Actively managed exchange-traded fund (ETF) — a basket of stocks that trades like a single stock. This one is a **long/short equity fund**, meaning it owns stocks it thinks will rise (long positions) and bets against stocks it thinks will fall (short positions — borrowing shares to sell, hoping to buy back lower).
- **Issuer / Advisor:** First Trust Advisors L.P.
- **Portfolio Managers:** John W. Gambla, CFA, and Rob A. Guttschow, CFA (Alternatives Investment Team)
- **Inception:** September 8, 2014
- **Current Price (NAV):** $72.78 (as of 4/24/2026); market price $72.88
- **Recent intraday close:** $72.28 (April 15, 2026)
- **AUM (Assets Under Management — total money in the fund):** ~$2.25 billion
- **Expense Ratio:** 1.38% per year (0.95% management fee + 0.43% other expenses such as the cost of borrowing shares to short)
- **Strategy in plain English:** Goes **long** stocks with high-quality earnings (real, durable profits — not accounting tricks) and **short** stocks with low-quality earnings. Typical net exposure is ~50–80% long (currently 96.17% long, -37.92% short, net 58.25%).

> **Important caveat for an inexperienced investor:** Warren Buffett's classic framework — moats, intrinsic value, decade-long holds — is built for evaluating a **single business**. FTLS is a fund holding ~395 positions that the managers rotate over time. Below, I adapt the lens: I evaluate the **strategy and the wrapper** (cost, manager skill, holdings quality) rather than a single-business moat.

### Moat Assessment
A moat is a durable advantage that protects profits from competitors. For an ETF, the closest equivalents are: cost advantage, scale, brand/distribution, and any process the manager runs that competitors cannot easily replicate.

- **Cost advantage: WEAK / NEGATIVE.** The 1.38% expense ratio is very high by ETF standards. The S&P 500 ETFs (SPY, VOO, IVV) charge 0.03%–0.09%. Even within long/short ETFs, 1.38% is on the higher end. Over a decade, fees compound: $10,000 invested loses roughly $1,500–$1,800 to fees (versus ~$80–$100 in an index fund). For Buffett, **fees are a permanent drag on the long-term compounding rate** — this is a real headwind to overcome.
- **Scale: MODERATE.** $2.25B AUM is healthy for a niche long/short product (many peers are sub-$500M and at risk of closure). Scale lowers per-share trading costs and signals investor trust.
- **Process moat: MIXED.** First Trust's earnings-quality approach (favoring companies whose reported profits convert to cash) is a respected academic factor (e.g., Sloan's accruals research). But the factor is well known and widely arbitraged — it is not a proprietary moat. Many quant shops run similar screens.
- **Brand/distribution: MODERATE.** First Trust is a well-established sponsor with broad advisor distribution; the fund earns a 4-star Morningstar rating over five years among 81 long-short equity peers — above-average peer ranking but not category-leading.
- **Holdings quality (the "underlying business" view):** The top long positions are textbook Buffett-friendly mega-caps with real moats — Apple, Microsoft, NVIDIA, Alphabet, JPMorgan, Berkshire Hathaway itself, Costco, Bank of America. **In other words, you're paying 1.38% to own roughly the S&P 500's largest names, plus a quality-tilt overlay and small short book.**

**Verdict on moat:** The strategy persistence is real (11+ year track record), but the **expense drag is the dominant negative**. There is no durable cost or process moat strong enough to justify paying ~1.30% above an index fund every year.

### Intrinsic Value Estimate
For an ETF, "intrinsic value" maps to two things:

1. **NAV vs. market price (premium/discount):** NAV (Net Asset Value — the per-share value of the fund's holdings) is $72.78 and market price is $72.88. **Premium of ~0.14%.** This is essentially fair — ETFs use creation/redemption to keep price near NAV. **No mispricing edge here.**

2. **Forward expected return after costs (the Buffett "owner earnings" analog):** The honest math for a long/short fund versus simply owning the market:

   - Long-run expected return of a roughly 60% net-long equity portfolio: ~6%–7% nominal (60% of an assumed 10–11% equity return).
   - Subtract the 1.38% expense ratio.
   - Subtract short-side costs (borrow fees, dividends paid on shorts) embedded in returns.
   - **Estimated net return to investor: ~5%–6% per year.**
   - Compare to an S&P 500 index fund at ~9%–10% per year long-run, gross of essentially-zero fees.

   The trade-off: you give up ~3–4 percentage points of expected return in exchange for **lower volatility and shallower drawdowns** (the drop from peak to trough). That is a legitimate trade for a risk-averse investor — but it is not a "compounder" Buffett would embrace.

3. **Underlying-holdings view:** The long book mirrors a slightly quality-tilted S&P 500. At spring 2026 levels, the S&P 500 trades at a forward P/E (price-to-earnings — the price per dollar of next year's expected profits) of roughly 21–22x, well above its long-run ~16x average. Buffett-style: **the underlying market is not cheap.**

### Margin of Safety
Margin of safety is the cushion between price and intrinsic value — Buffett famously demands ~25%+.

- **Premium to NAV:** essentially zero — neither cheap nor expensive on a wrapper basis.
- **Underlying market valuation:** elevated (forward P/E ~21–22x vs ~16x historical), so the long book has **negative** margin of safety relative to historical norms.
- **Fee headwind:** -1.38%/year — the equivalent of a permanent ~14% haircut over ten years that you must "earn back" before you've made anything.
- **Net:** **No meaningful margin of safety.** A Buffett investor would not accept this combination of full prices on the underlying + a high fee + no manager skill that has reliably beaten the long-short category recently.

### Management Quality
- **Portfolio managers:** John W. Gambla, CFA, and Rob A. Guttschow, CFA. Both are tenured senior portfolio managers on First Trust's Alternatives Investment Team and have run FTLS for many years (multi-year tenure in the same seat is a positive).
- **Capital allocation track record (manager skill):** 1-year NAV return 11.05%, 3-year annualized 12.92%, 10-year annualized 9.15%, since-inception 8.57%. Solid in absolute terms, but the fund **trailed the S&P 500 over most multi-year periods** (the index returned roughly 12–13% annualized over the past decade). FTLS's appeal is risk-adjusted, not absolute.
- **Recent results vs. peers:** 2024 was excellent (+18.80% vs category +13.85%), but 2025 lagged (+9.09% vs +10.08%) and YTD 2026 also trails (+1.04% vs +1.81%). Mixed.
- **Fund family reputation:** First Trust is a credible, mid-sized US ETF sponsor (~$200B+ across products). Not best-in-class on fees, but no scandals.
- **Distributions:** FTLS pays variable dividends (quarterly + year-end capital gains). The short book and active turnover can generate higher tax bills than a passive index fund — a hidden cost in taxable accounts.
- **Insider ownership / buybacks:** Not applicable to an ETF wrapper (no share buybacks; "insider ownership" would be First Trust employees holding the fund — not publicly disclosed).

**Manager verdict:** Competent, experienced team running a defensible quant process — but no evidence of repeatable alpha (excess return) sufficient to overcome the fee.

### Key Risks
1. **Fee compounding:** 1.38%/year is the single biggest threat to long-term wealth. Over 20 years, ~25% of potential gains go to expenses.
2. **Short-side risk:** Short positions can lose more than 100% (a stock can rise infinitely; a short bet has unlimited downside). Recent shorts include Oracle, Synopsys, Palo Alto Networks — high-quality tech names that could keep rising in an AI-driven bull market and hurt fund returns.
3. **Style drift / factor decay:** "Quality earnings" factors are well known and may deliver thinner premiums than they did historically as more capital chases them.
4. **Underperformance vs. simple alternatives:** A 60/40 mix of SPY and short-term Treasuries would likely match FTLS's net exposure with ~1.3% lower fees.
5. **Tax inefficiency:** Active trading + short-related ordinary income can make FTLS less efficient than index ETFs in taxable accounts.
6. **Manager / key-person risk:** Performance depends on Gambla and Guttschow continuing to refine the model.
7. **Bull-market drag:** In a strong, broad rally (like 2024), the short book can be a meaningful headwind.
8. **Closure risk:** Low — at $2.25B AUM, FTLS is far from the danger zone.

### Buffett-Lens Bottom Line
Buffett's preference is simple: **"If you don't have an edge, own the index."** FTLS is a thoughtful, well-run product, but it does not present a Buffett-style opportunity:

- The underlying long book is roughly the S&P 500's quality names — fine businesses, but at full prices and obtainable for nearly free elsewhere.
- The fee is a real, permanent drag with no demonstrated alpha to offset it.
- The wrapper trades at NAV — no discount.
- The underlying market lacks margin of safety at current valuations.

If you want **lower-volatility equity exposure**, FTLS is a reasonable specialty tool. If you want **a long-term compounder Buffett would buy and forget**, this is not it — a low-cost broad-market index fund or a single high-quality business at a fair price would be more consistent with the philosophy.

**Signal: Neutral (leaning bearish for a Buffett-style investor)**, primarily on fee and valuation grounds rather than any defect in the fund's execution.

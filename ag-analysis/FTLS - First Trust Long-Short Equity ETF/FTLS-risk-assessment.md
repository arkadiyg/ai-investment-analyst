**Reviewer:** Risk Manager, AG Capital

> **Quick glossary for the reader.**
> - **ETF** = Exchange-Traded Fund. A basket of holdings you buy as a single ticker.
> - **Long/Short Equity** = strategy that owns ("longs") some stocks expected to rise and bets against ("shorts") others expected to fall. Net exposure is long minus short.
> - **Beta** = how much a security tends to move when the broad market (S&P 500) moves 1%. Beta of 0.5 means about half the market's move; beta of 1.0 = same as market; beta of 1.5 = 50% more.
> - **Standard deviation (std dev)** = a measure of how much returns bounce around the average. Higher = more turbulent.
> - **Max drawdown (MaxDD)** = the worst peak-to-trough loss the fund has ever taken. The "how bad has it gotten" number.
> - **Sharpe ratio** = return per unit of risk taken. Higher is better. ~1.0 is solid; >1.5 is excellent.
> - **Alpha** = excess return vs. what you'd expect from market exposure alone. Positive alpha = the manager added value beyond simple beta.
> - **Correlation** = how closely two things move together. 1.0 = lockstep, 0.0 = unrelated, -1.0 = opposite.
> - **Expense ratio** = annual fee, expressed as % of assets. Comes out automatically — you never see a bill.

### Where the analysts agree (high consensus)

1. **The long book is high-quality but expensive** — Buffett, Growth, Fundamentals, and Sentiment all flag concentrated mega-cap tech (AAPL, MSFT, NVDA, GOOGL) at full valuations. Top-10 longs ≈ 22% of the fund.

3. **The strategy delivers on its mandate of damped volatility** — Fundamentals and Sentiment confirm; Buffett concedes the point. ~6.8% std dev vs SPY's ~15%, MaxDD -20.5% vs SPY -34% in 2020.
4. **Bull-market drag is the live problem** — short book + lower beta = under-performance when the tape rallies hard. Trailing 1-yr NAV +11.05% vs SPY +17.80%.

This is a clean **short-horizon vs long-horizon split**:

**What this split actually means:**
The tactical setup is constructive and the strategy is currently earning its keep. That does **not** resolve the long-term ownership-economics question. In other words: you can be right to hold it through 2026 and still be wrong to hold it through 2031. The decision is really *what is this position for in the portfolio?* — not *is the chart green?*

**One specific tail-risk on the bullish side:** Morningstar has flagged FTLS as "Average People / Average Process / lacks a durable edge." A future Morningstar downgrade could trigger model-portfolio reallocation away from FTLS by the very RIAs who are currently accumulating. This is the ~$110M-of-six-month-flows-running-in-reverse scenario. Watch for it.

## Recommended Position Size

**Recommendation: 5%–10% of total portfolio. Treat as a defensive equity sleeve, not a satellite alpha bet.**

### Why well under the 25% volatility-adjusted ceiling

The 25% ceiling is a *risk-budget cap*, not a sizing recommendation. Three reasons to size meaningfully below it:

1. **Three of five analysts are not bullish on multi-year ownership.** Position size should track conviction, and conviction here is moderate at best.
2. **FTLS is a strategy product, not an idiosyncratic alpha source.** Sizing it like a high-conviction equity bet (15%+) would over-weight a moderate-conviction holding.
3. **Correlation reality** — see next section. A larger position does not give you the diversification benefit the marketing implies.

### How to think about the position's role

- **5% sizing** = "small defensive ballast; I want some lower-vol exposure but I'm acknowledging the fee headwind."
- **10% sizing** = "core defensive sleeve; I am explicitly trading some upside for smaller drawdowns and I am OK paying 1.38% for that comfort."
- **15%+ sizing** = NOT recommended. At that weight you are paying ~21+ bps of fund fee on the *whole portfolio* per year for a strategy that is moderately rated by 3 of 5 analysts. The simple substitute (60% SPY + 40% short-term Treasuries) gets you similar net exposure for vastly less cost. If you want an actively managed L/S sleeve, fine — but cap it.

## Portfolio Risk Impact

**The honest correlation conversation:**

FTLS markets itself as a hedged / alternative equity product. It is. But its correlation to SPY is approximately **0.5–0.7** — meaningful, not low. That number matters because:

- A holding with 0.5–0.7 correlation to your main equity benchmark is **not a real diversifier away from US equity beta.** It dampens swings, but it does not protect you against the *kind* of risk a US-equity-heavy portfolio actually has.
- True diversification (negative or near-zero correlation) comes from things like long Treasuries, gold, managed futures, certain currencies, or genuinely uncorrelated alternatives — not from a 58%-net-long equity ETF.

**Concrete portfolio impact at different sizings (rule-of-thumb math):**

| FTLS weight | Effective added equity beta to portfolio | Net effect |
|---|---|---|
| 5% | +~0.025 | Negligible vol reduction; small fee cost |
| 10% | +~0.05 | Modest vol reduction; ~14 bps annual portfolio-level fee drag |
| 20% | +~0.10 | Visible vol reduction in drawdowns; ~28 bps annual fee drag, and you're now meaningfully underweighting cheap index beta |

**Substitution test (the question every analyst ran):**
A 60% SPY / 40% T-bill mix gets you a portfolio beta of ~0.60 — comparable to holding 100% FTLS — at roughly 5 bps blended cost vs. FTLS's 138 bps. The differentiator FTLS has to provide is **manager skill in the short book** (the 37.9% gross short position). The track record on that skill is mixed: alpha ~+1.16% but trailing SPY total return over 10 years.

**Sector concentration check:**
The long book overlaps heavily with any S&P 500 / large-cap-growth allocation already in the portfolio. If the existing portfolio holds VOO, IVV, QQQ, VTI, or large-cap tech individually, **FTLS adds factor concentration in mega-cap tech, not factor diversification.** The Portfolio Manager should net out the doubled exposure when sizing.

## Key Risk Factors (Ranked)

1. **Fee compounding (HIGHEST structural risk).** 1.38%/yr ≈ ~25% of cumulative gains over 20 years at typical equity returns. This risk is *certain* — it does not depend on markets, it just happens.
2. **Bull-market drag.** If SPY resumes a strong rally, the short book (-37.9% gross) becomes a headwind and FTLS will visibly lag. This is the most common complaint pattern from holders.
3. **Manager / factor decay risk.** The "quality earnings" factor is widely arbitraged. If the screening edge erodes, alpha goes to zero and you are left holding a high-fee S&P-lite.
4. **Mega-cap tech factor concentration.** Top-10 longs ~22%, dominated by AAPL/MSFT/NVDA/GOOGL. If mega-cap tech corrects sharply, the long book takes a direct hit; the short book typically does not fully offset.
5. **Tax inefficiency in taxable accounts.** 245% turnover → meaningful short-term cap gains. Effectively adds 30–50 bps to all-in cost in a taxable account.
6. **Morningstar downgrade tail event.** Current rating is 4-star with an "Average" qualitative read. A formal downgrade could trigger model-portfolio reallocation by the RIAs who currently account for the bulk of the +$110M six-month flows — flow reversal risk.
7. **Substitution / fee compression risk.** Peer L/S ETFs at 0.69–0.95% (Calamos CLSE, others) are emerging. If allocators decide cheaper is good enough, FTLS faces structural AUM pressure.
8. **Liquidity in stress.** AUM ~$2.2B and tight spreads under normal conditions, but L/S strategies can experience wider spreads during volatility spikes when their short books need to be rebalanced. Use limit orders in stressed tape.

### Tail events the Portfolio Manager should pre-think

- **Sharp mega-cap tech reversal** (e.g., AI-capex unwind): long book hit hard; short book unlikely to fully offset; expect FTLS to draw down 60–80% as much as SPY.
- **Sustained low-vol bull tape** (e.g., AI-driven rally continues 2+ years): FTLS underperforms by 400–800 bps/year cumulatively; holder fatigue triggers redemptions.
- **Genuine market crash** (-30%+ SPY drawdown): FTLS's best historical scenario — likely down ~15–20%, which is when you'll be glad you owned it. This is the one regime where the fee is clearly worth it.

## Risk Flags for the Portfolio Manager

The Portfolio Manager should weigh these heavily in the final allocation decision:

1. **DO NOT confuse "low volatility" with "diversifier."** FTLS is 0.5–0.7 correlated to SPY. It is a smoother equity sleeve, not a hedge.
2. **DO NOT chase the price at $72.88.** Reward/risk at the high is poor. Either wait for a pullback to $71–$72 or a confirmed breakout above $73.15 on volume.
3. **CAP the position at 10%.** The 25% volatility-adjusted ceiling is a budget, not a target. Conviction (avg confidence 61) and the existence of cheap substitutes argue for material under-sizing.
4. **PLACE in tax-advantaged accounts** if at all possible. 245% turnover quietly adds 30–50 bps of tax drag in taxable accounts.
5. **NET against existing US large-cap exposure.** If the portfolio already owns VOO/IVV/QQQ/VTI or individual mega-cap tech, FTLS overlaps significantly. Adjust other large-cap weights downward if FTLS goes in.
6. **WATCH the Morningstar review cycle.** A downgrade from current 4-star/"Average" rating is the single biggest discrete flow-reversal risk and could move price 3–6% in days.
7. **REVIEW thesis if 1-month outflows continue or accelerate.** The -$7.3M one-month flow is the first crack in a 5-year accumulation story. Two consecutive quarters of net outflows = thesis-broken signal; reassess sizing.
8. **HONESTLY acknowledge the substitution test.** If the answer to "why not 60% SPY + 40% T-bills?" is not crisp and conviction-backed, the position is not justified. The L/S wrapper has to earn its 135 bps.

### Bottom line for the Portfolio Manager

FTLS is a defensible, well-run, low-volatility equity sleeve — appropriate as a 5–10% holding in a tax-advantaged account for an investor who explicitly values smaller drawdowns over maximum upside and is comfortable paying 1.38% for that. It is **not** a high-conviction alpha bet, **not** a portfolio diversifier in the strict sense, and **not** appropriate at the 25% volatility-budget ceiling. Size it for what it is: defensive ballast.

## Fundamentals Analyst Signal Report — FTLS

**Signal:** neutral (with a modest bullish lean on risk-adjusted basis)
**Confidence:** 60

> Note for the reader: FTLS is an ETF, not an operating company. Classical fundamentals (revenue, EPS, free cash flow) do not apply directly. Below I translate the standard fundamentals framework into ETF-appropriate metrics: cost of ownership (expense ratio), portfolio construction quality (long/short exposure, turnover), the fundamentals of the *underlying* stocks the fund holds long, and risk-adjusted historical performance. Plain-English definitions are inline.

---

### Security Identification

- **Name:** First Trust Long/Short Equity ETF
- **Ticker:** FTLS (NYSE Arca)
- **Type:** Actively managed long/short equity ETF (open-end, 1940-Act)
- **Issuer / Adviser:** First Trust Advisors L.P.
- **Inception:** September 8, 2014 (~11.5-year track record)
- **Strategy in plain English:** The fund buys ("goes long") U.S.-listed stocks the manager believes will outperform, and sells short (bets against) U.S.-listed stocks the manager believes will underperform. The goal is equity-like returns with smaller drawdowns than the S&P 500. Under normal conditions it is 90–100% long and 0–50% short.
- **Current size:** ~**$2.18–2.25 B AUM**; **NAV ≈ $72.78** (early April 2026)
- **Holdings count:** ~395 securities across long and short books

---

### Profitability Metrics (translated for an ETF)

For an ETF, "profitability" really means: how profitable are the *companies* the fund owns long, and how much income does the fund itself distribute to shareholders?

- **Long-book quality:** The long book is anchored in U.S. mega-cap quality compounders — Apple, Microsoft, NVIDIA, Alphabet, JPMorgan, ASML, Bank of America, Berkshire Hathaway, Citigroup, Costco. These names, in aggregate, carry blended **ROE in the 20–30% range** and **operating margins meaningfully above the S&P 500 average** (mega-cap tech alone runs 30%+ operating margins). This is a high-quality long sleeve.
- **Sector mix of longs:** Information Technology ~25%, Financials ~19%, Consumer Discretionary ~9%. That is a quality-and-cyclical tilt — profitable, cash-generative businesses.
- **Distribution yield (the cash the ETF pays you):** **12-month distribution rate ≈ 0.96%**, **30-day SEC yield ≈ 0.11%**. ("SEC yield" = a standardized estimate of forward income net of expenses; near-zero here because the 1.38% expense ratio eats almost all the dividend income from holdings.)
- **Read-through:** The underlying long-book companies are highly profitable, but very little of that profitability reaches shareholders as cash distributions because (a) the fund's holdings are mostly low-yielding growth names, and (b) the expense ratio absorbs most of the residual income.

### Growth Metrics (translated)

- **AUM growth:** FTLS has scaled to ~$2.2B, materially larger than most long/short ETF peers — a sign of durable demand and operational viability. (Many long/short ETFs sit below $200M.)
- **NAV CAGR since inception (Sep 2014 → Apr 2026, ~11.5 yrs):** ~**8.5–8.6% annualized** since inception per the issuer's data; **~9.0% per year over the last 10 years** per third-party data. This is solid in absolute terms but trails the S&P 500's ~12.2% annualized over the same window — expected, because a partially-hedged fund is structurally designed to give up upside in exchange for lower drawdowns.
- **Distribution growth:** Distributions are small and uneven (most recent ≈ $0.0634/share). This is not a yield vehicle.
- **Recent performance:** YTD 2026 ≈ **-0.97%**; trailing 1-year ≈ **+11.05%**; trailing 3-year ≈ **+12.92%** annualized. Calendar-year 2025 returned ~9.09%, which slightly trailed the long/short category average (~10.08%).

### Balance Sheet / Exposure Health

This is the ETF analogue to a company's balance sheet — how the book is constructed and how much risk it carries.

- **Gross long exposure:** ~**96.2%** of NAV
- **Gross short exposure:** ~**-37.9%** of NAV
- **Net market exposure:** ~**58.3%** (long minus short). Plain English: For every $100 invested in FTLS, the fund effectively has ~$58 of net stock-market exposure — about 58% of an unhedged S&P 500 ETF.
- **Gross exposure:** ~**134.1%** (long plus absolute short). This is the total notional capital "at work" — moderate leverage. Pure long-only ETFs run at 100% gross.
- **Beta to S&P 500:** ~**0.51–0.53** (5-yr monthly). Plain English: When the S&P moves 1%, FTLS tends to move about half a percent.
- **Cash / leverage profile:** No structural debt; short proceeds and a modest cash sleeve fund the short book. Leverage is operational, not financial.
- **Read-through:** The book is conservatively constructed. The 58% net exposure is the dominant risk factor, and it's lower than most "long-biased" hedge funds. This is a defensive equity wrapper, not an aggressive alpha bet.

---

### Cash Flow Quality

For an ETF, "cash flow" maps to: does the income the underlying portfolio generates cover what the fund pays out, and are distributions sustainable?

- **Distributions are tiny relative to expenses.** With a 1.38% expense ratio and a 30-day SEC yield of ~0.11%, the ETF is consuming nearly all of its portfolio's net income to fund operations. There is no "yield cushion."
- **Sustainability:** Distributions are small but easily covered by portfolio income plus realized gains. There is no risk of return-of-capital distributions becoming a structural problem.
- **Tax efficiency caveat:** Portfolio turnover is **~245% (as of 3/31/2026)** — extremely high. ("Turnover" measures how much of the portfolio is replaced in a year; 100% means the entire book changes hands once.) High turnover plus a short book typically means more short-term realized gains, which the ETF in-kind redemption mechanism only partially mitigates because shorts cannot be delivered in-kind. **Expect periodic capital-gains distributions**, which are a real after-tax drag in a taxable account.
- **Read-through:** Cash flow quality is acceptable but unremarkable — this is a total-return vehicle, not an income vehicle, and it is **better held in a tax-advantaged account** (IRA, 401(k)) than a taxable one.

---

### Valuation Assessment

For an ETF, "valuation" is a three-part question: (1) what does it cost to own, (2) what are the underlying stocks valued at, and (3) is the ETF trading at its fair NAV in the market?

**1. Expense ratio vs peers**
- FTLS net expense ratio: **1.38%** (1.18% management fee + ~0.20% from short-borrow / dividend expense on shorts).
- US News / category data: FTLS is reportedly **~36% below the long/short equity category average** expense ratio. Long/short ETFs are inherently expensive — peers commonly run 1.5–2.5%. So while 1.38% is high *vs. a long-only S&P 500 fund (0.03%)*, it is **competitive within the L/S ETF universe**.
- The honest comparison: an investor paying 1.38% to capture roughly half the market's beta needs the manager's stock selection to add real alpha versus simply allocating ~58% to a 0.03%-fee S&P 500 fund and 42% to T-bills. The historical record (~9% per year, ~0.5 beta, smaller drawdowns) supports the case but not overwhelmingly.

**2. Long-book weighted multiples (roughly)**
With AAPL, MSFT, NVDA, GOOGL, AMZN, META, AVGO at the top of the long book in a tech-heavy ~25% IT weight, the long-sleeve weighted **P/E sits roughly in the high-20s to low-30s**, **P/S ~5–7x**, modestly **above the S&P 500's ~22–24x P/E**. The long book is *not cheap* — it is quality-at-a-premium. The short book partially offsets valuation risk by betting against weaker-fundamentals or richly-valued names.

**3. Premium/Discount to NAV**
- Current premium/discount: **~0.00–0.11%** — effectively trading at NAV. Median 30-day bid/ask spread ~0.26%. Liquidity is good. **No premium/discount red flag.**

### Earnings / Holdings Quality Notes

Risk flags and structural observations for an inexperienced investor:

- **Concentration risk in the long book is moderate:** Top 10 longs ≈ **22.1%** of fund assets. That's well-diversified at the issuer level, but the *factor* concentration in mega-cap tech is real — if the top 6 tech names underperform, FTLS will underperform.
- **High turnover (~245%) is a yellow flag.** It signals tactical management and creates tax friction. The manager is actively repositioning, not buy-and-hold.
- **Short-book opacity.** The fund discloses long holdings but the short-book composition by sector is not granularly published in real time. Investors are taking on faith that the manager's short selection adds value. Long/short funds historically have a mixed record on this.
- **Manager-skill dependency.** Unlike a passive index ETF, performance hinges on First Trust's stock-selection process. The 11.5-year track record (~8.6% CAGR with ~20.5% max drawdown vs. the S&P's -34% peak drawdown in 2020) is a credible proof-of-concept but trails the index on total return.
- **Risk-adjusted strengths:** **3-yr Sharpe ratio ~1.15**, **3-yr standard deviation only ~6.8%** (vs. ~15% for SPY), **alpha ~+1.16%**. ("Sharpe" = excess return per unit of volatility; >1.0 is generally good.) On a risk-adjusted basis, FTLS has delivered.
- **Max drawdown ~-20.5%** (March 2020) — meaningfully better than SPY's -33.99% in the same drawdown.

---

### Summary Judgment

**Why neutral (not bullish):**
1. Expense ratio of 1.38% is a real and permanent drag — cheap vs. L/S peers, expensive vs. plain index funds.
2. Long-term total return (~9% CAGR) trails the S&P 500 (~12% CAGR) over 10 years, so the fund only "wins" if you value drawdown protection and lower volatility.
3. High turnover creates tax inefficiency in taxable accounts.
4. Distribution yield is effectively zero after fees.

**Why not bearish:**
1. 3-year Sharpe of ~1.15 with ~6.8% volatility and ~0.5 beta is genuinely attractive risk-adjusted performance.
2. Smaller drawdowns (~-20% vs. -34% for SPY) means it does what it advertises — equity participation with damped downside.
3. Trades at NAV with tight spreads and a $2.2B AUM base — institutional-grade liquidity.
4. Long-book quality (mega-cap tech + financials) is fundamentally strong.

**Best-fit investor:** Risk-conscious investor seeking ~half the equity beta with smaller drawdowns, holding inside a tax-advantaged account. **Not a fit for** investors who want maximum upside, want yield, or want the lowest-cost option.

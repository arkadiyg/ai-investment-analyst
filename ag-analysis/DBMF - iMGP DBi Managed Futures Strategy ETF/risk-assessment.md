## Risk Manager Assessment — DBMF

**Security:** iMGP DBi Managed Futures Strategy ETF (DBMF)
**Verified Price:** $30.51 (Morningstar, 2026-04-25)
**AUM:** ~$3.2–3.5B • **Expense Ratio:** 0.85% • **Inception:** May 2019
**Compiled:** 2026-04-27

> **Plain-English glossary (for the new investor):**
> - **Managed futures:** A strategy that buys or sells (long or short) liquid futures contracts on stocks, bonds, currencies, and commodities, following price trends. It can profit when markets fall, which is why it's used as a "diversifier."
> - **Correlation:** How closely two things move together. **+1** = perfect lockstep, **0** = unrelated, **-1** = perfect opposite.
> - **Tracking error:** The gap between a fund's return and the benchmark it's trying to replicate. Persistent tracking error means the fund's "promise" is slipping.
> - **Trend decay:** Multi-year stretches where price trends are choppy, not directional, and trend-following strategies make little or nothing.
> - **Sharpe ratio:** Return per unit of risk taken. Higher = more efficient use of risk.
> - **Crisis alpha:** Tendency of managed futures to make money when stocks/bonds are falling together.

1. **Buffett (Neutral, 60) vs Growth + Sentiment (Bullish, 78/72)** → **horizon question.** Buffett asks "does this belong in a multi-decade portfolio?" and answers ambivalently — managed futures is a zero-sum trading activity, not a compounder of intrinsic value. Translation for a beginner: the fund had a great year, and one model thinks the easy money is in the rear-view mirror over the next quarter.

It missed by ~500 bps in *both* 2024 and 2025. Yet investors are pouring money in because the *wrapper* (DBMF the ETF) is up. If the Dynamic Beta Engine's 60-day regression keeps lagging on regime shifts, the strategy's core selling point erodes — even as assets accumulate. Flows can be a lagging indicator.

### Volatility Classification

**Classification: MEDIUM (with important caveats)**

- **Realized monthly volatility:** ~11.9% multi-year (lower than equity ~15%, similar to a balanced portfolio)
- **Maximum drawdown:** historically modest in absolute terms
- **Correlation to SPY:** ~ -0.1 to +0.2 (a *real* diversifier — uncorrelated to mildly-anticorrelated)

By the framework's pure vol axis, DBMF would qualify as **LOW (max 25%)**. We are deliberately classifying it **MEDIUM** instead, and **the reason is not monthly volatility — it's strategy-decay risk over multi-year periods.**

> **Why we override down the vol class:** Managed futures has historically had **5+ year stretches of flat or negative real returns** (e.g., 2013–2018, 2009–2012). These are not "high-vol" periods — they are slow-bleed periods. A naive vol-only classifier under-prices that risk. The investor experience of "owning a 12% vol fund that goes nowhere for 5 years while equities triple" is psychologically devastating and often produces a sale at the worst time.

So: **MEDIUM in framework terms, with the explicit understanding that the binding risk is multi-year trend decay, not monthly drawdowns.**

### Recommended Position Size

**Framework cap (Medium vol class): 15% of total portfolio**
**Recommended starting size: 4–6%**
**Hard ceiling under any condition: 8%**

**Justification:**

The framework cap (15%) is the *vol-adjusted ceiling*; it is not the right starting size for this fund. Three reasons we recommend tighter than the cap:

1. **Strategy-decay risk binds tighter than monthly vol.** Multi-year trend droughts have happened twice in the last 17 years. A 4–6% sleeve survives a 5-year drought without forcing a behavioral sale; a 15% sleeve probably does not.
2. **No margin of safety on entry.** Per Buffett analyst: trades at NAV after a +26% trailing year. Sizing up after a vintage year is the opposite of disciplined entry.
3. **Cohort-C panic risk is a wrapper-level fragility.** Per Sentiment: April 2026 first-time buyers are likely to panic-sell on the first 8–12% pullback. Heavy retail-chasing flows often unwind violently. We don't want our position size to depend on other holders' staying power.

**The portfolio role honestly stated:** DBMF is a **genuine diversifier** with low/negative equity correlation (-0.1 to +0.2 to SPY) — *unlike FTLS, which is essentially an equity sleeve dressed up as a hedge*. This is DBMF's core appeal. A 4–6% sleeve gives meaningful diversification benefit (some negative-correlation contribution to portfolio variance) without betting the farm on the asset class continuing to deliver.

**Scaling protocol (suggested):** start at 4%, add a 1–2% increment only after either (a) a 10–15% pullback that resolves the "no margin of safety" concern, or (b) a 12-month period during which the Dynamic Beta Engine's tracking error vs SG CTA Index narrows back to ≤200 bps.

### Portfolio Risk Impact

Adding a 4–6% DBMF sleeve to a typical equity-heavy portfolio has these effects:

- **Reduces total portfolio variance** modestly. With correlation to SPY between -0.1 and +0.2, DBMF's risk contribution is much less than its weight would suggest in a standard variance calculation. (Beginner translation: because DBMF zigs when stocks zag, it cancels some of the portfolio's wiggle.)
- **Improves portfolio Sharpe ratio** in most historical regimes — the diversification benefit is real, not accounting fiction. This is the genuine appeal.
- **Provides crisis-alpha optionality.** If equities and bonds fall together (the 2022 scenario), DBMF historically goes up. That's the asymmetric payoff the sleeve is buying.
- **Drag in equity bull markets.** Be honest: in a sustained equity rally, DBMF is more likely to underperform SPY by a wide margin. The investor must be willing to "look stupid" for 3–5 years at a stretch in exchange for the crisis-alpha behavior.

**Net judgment:** DBMF is doing real diversification work, not cosmetic diversification. This is precisely the role that distinguishes it from equity-correlated hedges (e.g., long/short equity ETFs like FTLS, which look like hedges but behave like equity sleeves).

### Key Risk Factors (ranked, most important first)

1. **Trend-regime decay (multi-year flat/negative returns).** Historical precedent: 2013–2018, 2009–2012. This is the strategy-level tail, not the monthly tail.
2. **Two-year tracking error vs SG CTA Index.** -500 bps in both 2024 and 2025. The Dynamic Beta Engine's 60-day regression appears to lag at regime shifts. If this persists, the fund's defining promise (cheap replication) is compromised.
3. **Fee compression from SDMF (Simplify, launched Nov 2025) and iShares ISMF.** Likely 0.50–0.65% all-in for explicit DBMF clones. Material 24-month threat to economics and possibly to fund flows.
4. **Cohort-C behavioral risk.** April 2026 first-time buyers are flow-chasing after a strong year. Likely to panic-sell on first 8–12% pullback. This affects the wrapper's stability, not the strategy itself.
5. **Holder behavioral risk (general).** Managed futures lags in equity bull markets. The modal scenario is 3–5 years of looking dumb relative to SPY. Many investors abandon at the wrong time.
6. **Capacity / slippage at $3.5B+.** The strategy uses only ~10 liquid futures contracts. Big AUM relative to a small instrument set creates execution drag at scale.
7. **Tax inefficiency in taxable accounts.** Distributions are lumpy (realized cap gains + T-bill interest), partially offset by §1256 60/40 treatment (~27% blended federal vs 37% ordinary).
8. **Andrew Beer key-person risk.** Recognized face of the strategy and firm. Departure or health event would be a meaningful sentiment shock.
9. **Crowding / similar-positioning risk.** When managed-futures positioning gets one-sided, unwind moves are sharp.

### Risk Flags (what the Portfolio Manager should weigh heavily)

- **The horizon split is the central judgment call.** Buffett's "not a compounder" critique and Growth/Sentiment's "next 1–3 years look great" view are both correct on their own terms. The Portfolio Manager has to decide which horizon governs the AG Capital portfolio.
- **Do NOT size to the framework cap (15%).** The vol class is medium, but the binding risk is multi-year strategy decay, which monthly vol does not capture. Recommend 4–6% start, 8% hard ceiling.
- **Treat the 2-year tracking error as a yellow flag, not a green light.** Even bullish flows don't fix the fact that the strategy's core promise (cheap replication of SG CTA Index) has been slipping for two years running. Re-evaluate the position if 2026 also closes with >300 bps tracking gap.
- **Plan the entry, not just the size.** No margin of safety after +26% trailing year. Consider a phased entry (e.g., 2% now, +1% on a 5–8% pullback, +1% more on a tracking-error improvement).
- **DBMF's role is genuinely different from FTLS.** Correlation -0.1 to +0.2 to SPY is real diversification. Do not double-up "hedge" exposure between the two and assume similar behavior — they are not similar instruments.
- **Watch SDMF flows and ER cuts as a leading indicator.** If Simplify's clone gathers $500M+ in 12 months, expect DBi to cut DBMF's fee, which is good for the holder but compresses the fund's economics and may slow future product investment.
- **Rebalance discipline matters more than entry timing.** With low equity correlation, DBMF will drift in weight independent of equities. Set explicit rebalance bands (e.g., trim above 7%, add below 3%) and stick to them. This is how the sleeve actually delivers its theoretical diversification benefit.
- **Behavioral pre-commitment.** Whoever owns this position must commit, in writing, to *not* selling during a 3–5 year underperformance vs SPY. If the holder cannot honestly commit to that, the position should not be opened — managed futures is uniquely punishing to investors who buy after big years and sell after small ones.

---

**Risk Manager bottom line:** DBMF is a high-quality vehicle in a structurally useful asset class, available at a fair-not-cheap price, after a great year. The framework permits up to 15%; we recommend starting at 4–6% with an 8% hard ceiling, phased entry, and explicit pre-commitment to weather a multi-year trend drought. The diversification benefit is real (this is its key advantage over equity-correlated hedges); the strategy-decay tail is also real. Sized correctly, this is a legitimate sleeve. Sized to the cap, it becomes a behavioral landmine.
